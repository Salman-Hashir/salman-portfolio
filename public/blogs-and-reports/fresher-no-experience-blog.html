Why Freshers With No Experience
Can't Get Experience

The loop that traps millions of graduates — and nobody is talking about it honestly.

By Salman Hashir  ·  Kerala → Dubai  ·  IT Professional  ·  ~1300 words

Congratulations! You just finished your degree or diploma. You are qualified, motivated, and ready to take on the world. Now go apply for a job that requires 2–3 years of experience. Good luck. 🙂
· · ·

// THE PROBLEMThe Most Absurd Loop in the History of Employment

At some point in human history, someone in an HR department decided that the best way to hire new people was to only hire people who had already been hired. Genius. Truly. A masterclass in circular logic.

Here's exactly how it goes every single time:

The Fresher Experience Paradox™

Graduate 🎓
→
Apply for jobs
→
"Need 2 yrs exp"
→
Rejected
→
Apply again 😅
↩
"We are looking for a fresh graduate with 3 years of industry experience, proficiency in 12 programming languages, and a passion for working weekends. Stipend: ₹5,000/month. Apply now!"

Sound familiar? Because it is. This is the daily reality for millions of IT graduates across India — and honestly, across the world. You spend years studying, you get your certificate, and then the industry looks at you and says: "Great. But have you done this professionally before?"

No. That's literally why I'm applying. That's what a fresher means.

// THE IRONYWhy Does This Even Happen?

Companies are terrified of training people. They want someone who can sit down on Day 1 and immediately contribute — zero hand-holding, zero learning curve. This sounds reasonable until you realize that every single experienced person on earth was once a fresher who someone took a chance on.

The experienced engineer you're hiring today? Someone gave them their first job once. Someone dealt with their mistakes, their questions, their slow start. And now that engineer is "experienced." But nobody wants to be the one who does the investing. Everyone wants to harvest from a tree they didn't plant.

HR logic: "We can't hire you because you have no experience." Also HR: "How are you supposed to get experience?" HR: *disappears into the void*

// MY STORYHow I Broke the Loop — The Honest Version

📍 Personal Experience — Salman Hashir, Kerala

I'm not going to pretend I cracked some secret code. Honestly? I just took whatever was in front of me and made it count.

Step 1 — IT Instructor Apprenticeship (2 Months)
My first role was an IT Instructor apprenticeship at a school. Stipend: ₹8,000 a month. I worked there for 2 months — not glamorous, but it gave me something no classroom ever did: real responsibility. Explaining concepts, troubleshooting live problems, showing up with a purpose every day. That built confidence. No experience certificate given. No formal proof on paper. Just skills in my head and hands.
Step 2 — Tech Support Engineer at Asianet (10 Months)
Through an Employment Exchange interview — yes, the old-school government job board — I got selected at Asianet as a Tech Support Engineer. Real company, real customers, real pressure. I worked there for 10 months, learned a lot, grew fast. And then — no experience certificate from here either. Nearly a year of genuine work, and I walked away with skills but no official paper to prove it. Total verifiable Indian IT experience: zero on paper. Twelve months in reality.
Step 3 — Dubai (Currently Searching)
I made the move to Dubai for better opportunities — as thousands of Keralites do every year. And here's what nobody warns you about: if you have verified Indian experience, you'll at least get interview calls in the UAE. If you don't have a certificate or a reference, you need people to recommend you. Without either? You're starting from zero. That's exactly where I am right now. Still searching. Still going.

The honest moral of my story isn't a feel-good ending — not yet. It's a warning: always collect your experience certificate, even from short stints. Even if the job paid poorly. Even if you only stayed 2 months. That paper becomes your passport when you're standing in a foreign country trying to prove you exist professionally.

// THE SOLUTIONHow to Actually Break the Loop (Without a Time Machine)

Okay, enough venting. Here's what actually works — things I wish someone had told me clearly when I was staring at rejection emails:

🐙
GitHub & Open Source

Your GitHub profile IS your resume. Build projects, contribute to open source, push code regularly. A recruiter who sees real work will overlook a lack of job titles every time.

🤝
Internships & Volunteering

Even unpaid or low-paid work counts as experience. Schools, NGOs, small businesses — offer to handle their IT. You learn, they benefit, you get something to put on your CV.

💻
Freelancing

Fiverr, Upwork, local Facebook groups. Fix someone's WordPress site, set up a network, do tech support remotely. Clients don't care about your degree. They care if you solve the problem.

📜
Certifications

CompTIA A+, CCNA, AWS Cloud Practitioner, Google IT Support — these are internationally recognized proof of skill. In the UAE especially, certs often matter more than your degree.

💼
LinkedIn — But Do It Right

Don't just create a profile and wait. Post what you're learning. Comment on industry posts. Connect with people in your target companies. Recruiters in the UAE actively scout LinkedIn. Your activity signals that you're engaged and serious — even before your first job title.

// THE TRUTHThe System Is Broken — But You Can Still Win

Let's be honest: the hiring system is not designed for freshers. It never really was. It's designed for companies to minimize risk, minimize training cost, and maximize output from day one. That's the machine.

But here's what the machine doesn't account for: people who find side doors. Every person reading this who took a low-paying apprenticeship, did a free internship, built a project nobody asked for, or got certified on their own time — you found the side door. And once you're inside, nobody asks how you got in.

I went from an ₹8,000/month school apprenticeship to a tech support role at a major Kerala company — and now I'm in Dubai, navigating a market that demands either verifiable experience or someone willing to vouch for you. I have neither right now. I'm telling you this not for sympathy, but because this is the reality thousands of us face — and pretending otherwise helps nobody.

If there's one thing I'd go back and change: get that experience certificate. From every job. No matter how short. No matter how small. Those papers travel with you across borders in ways that memories and skills alone cannot.

The loop only traps you if you play by its rules. Build things. Get certified. Take the small job. Show up. The experience you need is closer than you think — it just doesn't come with a job title yet.
