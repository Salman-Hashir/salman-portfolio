Introduction

Have you ever wondered what really happens inside a computer when you type, game, edit videos, or browse the web? Every task you perform — from opening a browser tab to rendering a 3D scene — is powered by a set of carefully engineered hardware components working in harmony.
Building or buying a PC can feel overwhelming at first. Walk into any computer store or browse any tech website and you are immediately confronted with a tsunami of acronyms: CPU, GPU, DDR5, NVMe, PCIe, TDP, 80+ Gold, LGA1700... It can feel like a foreign language.
This guide is written for you — the beginner, the curious learner, the person who wants to understand what they are paying for. By the end of this article, you will understand every major component inside a modern PC, how they interact with each other, and how to pick the right combination for your needs and budget.
// Ch. 01

The Processor (CPU)

The Central Processing Unit (CPU) is the brain of your computer. Every instruction your software gives — from adding two numbers to rendering a game frame — passes through the CPU. It reads instructions, processes them, and sends results to other components.
Modern CPUs are incredibly complex chips containing billions of transistors packed into a piece of silicon roughly the size of a postage stamp. Despite their tiny physical size, they are capable of processing billions of operations every single second.
How CPUs Work
A CPU works on a cycle called Fetch-Decode-Execute. It fetches an instruction from memory, decodes what that instruction means, and then executes it. This happens millions of times per second, per core. The speed at which this cycle happens is measured in GHz (gigahertz) — one GHz equals one billion cycles per second.
Modern CPUs do not just have one processing unit — they have multiple cores. Each core can handle its own set of instructions independently, making multi-core CPUs dramatically more efficient for tasks that can be split across parallel workloads.
Intel vs AMD
Today, the desktop CPU market is dominated by two companies: Intel and AMD. Intel produces the Core series — Core i3, i5, i7, and i9. AMD produces the Ryzen series — Ryzen 3, 5, 7, and 9. Both companies offer excellent processors. The choice often comes down to budget, platform features, and intended use.

Key CPU Specs Explained
Cores & Threads
Physical cores handle tasks; threads (via Hyperthreading/SMT) double virtual cores per chip for parallel workloads.
Clock Speed (GHz)
Measures cycles per second. Boost clocks provide temporary speed spikes when temperatures allow.
Cache (L1/L2/L3)
Ultra-fast on-chip memory. L1 is smallest and fastest; L3 is largest and still far faster than RAM.
TDP (Watts)
Maximum heat generated under load. A 125W TDP chip needs more cooling and power than a 65W chip.

The Motherboard

The motherboard is the main circuit board of your PC — the backbone that connects every component together. Your CPU, RAM, GPU, storage, and all other parts plug into or connect through the motherboard. Think of it as the nervous system of your computer.
Choosing the right motherboard matters enormously because it determines what CPU you can use, how much RAM you can install, what features your system will support, and how much you can upgrade in the future.

Key Features
Most ATX boards offer 4 RAM slots. M.2 slots accept NVMe SSDs directly on the motherboard without cables. The VRM (Voltage Regulator Module) delivers clean power to the CPU — for overclocking or high-TDP processors, a strong VRM is essential. Budget boards may have weaker VRMs unsuitable for power-hungry chips.

RAM — Random Access Memory

RAM is your computer's short-term memory — a fast, temporary workspace where your CPU stores data it is actively working with. Unlike storage drives, RAM is volatile: all data in RAM is erased when you power off your PC.
More RAM means you can have more applications open simultaneously and handle larger datasets without slowdown. For most users, 16GB is the sweet spot in 2025, while content creators and power users benefit from 32GB or more.

The Graphics Card (GPU)

The Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) is a specialized processor designed to handle visual and parallel computing tasks. While a CPU excels at sequential tasks with a few powerful cores, a GPU contains thousands of smaller, simpler cores designed to handle many operations simultaneously.
For gamers, the GPU is arguably the most important component — it renders every frame of every game. For video editors and 3D artists, GPU acceleration dramatically speeds up rendering and effects processing.
NVIDIA vs AMD
NVIDIA GeForce RTX 30 and 40 series lead in ray tracing, DLSS AI upscaling, and content creation workloads. AMD Radeon RX 7000 series offers excellent rasterization performance and often better value, with open-source FSR upscaling technology.

Key GPU Specs
VRAM (Video RAM) stores textures and frame buffers on the GPU. Modern games at 1080p need 8GB minimum; 4K gaming and content creation require 12–24GB. Running out of VRAM causes severe performance drops. GPUs connect via PCIe x16 slots and high-end cards can consume 100W to 450W.

Power Supply (PSU / SMPS)

The Switch Mode Power Supply (SMPS) — commonly called a PSU — converts AC power from your wall outlet into the DC voltages your PC components need. A poor quality PSU can damage other components. A PSU that is too weak will cause system instability, crashes, and shutdowns.

The golden rule: choose a PSU with at least 20–25% more wattage than your estimated consumption. This headroom ensures stability, efficiency, and longevity. Never cheap out on a power supply — it protects every other component in your system.

Storage — SSD, NVMe & HDD

Storage is where your operating system, applications, games, and files permanently live. Unlike RAM, storage retains data when the power is off. Modern PCs use several types of storage, each with different speeds, capacities, and price points.

Cooling Systems

Every component in your PC generates heat. If that heat is not removed efficiently, temperatures rise until the CPU or GPU throttles itself — reducing performance to protect from damage. In extreme cases, overheating causes system crashes or permanent hardware damage.

Compatibility — Making It Work

Every component in your build must be compatible with every other. The CPU socket must match the motherboard socket exactly. An AMD Ryzen 7000 CPU (AM5 socket) will not fit in a board with an AM4 socket, even if it looks similar.
CPU + Motherboard
Socket must match exactly — AM5 ≠ AM4, LGA1700 ≠ LGA1851.
RAM Type
DDR4 and DDR5 slots are physically different and not interchangeable.
M.2 NVMe Gen
PCIe 4.0 NVMe in a PCIe 3.0 slot works at half speed. Check specs.
PSU Wattage
Add CPU + GPU TDP + 50–100W for other components, then add 25% headroom.
Case Form Factor
ATX, mATX, and Mini-ITX are not interchangeable. Also check GPU length and cooler height.
PCIe Backward Compat.
PCIe is backward/forward compatible — a 4.0 GPU works in a 3.0 slot with minor bandwidth limits.

Conclusion

Building or buying a PC is one of the most rewarding technology investments you can make. Understanding each component — the CPU, motherboard, RAM, GPU, PSU, storage, and cooling — gives you the power to make informed decisions rather than blindly trusting a pre-built spec sheet.
Whether you are building your first PC, upgrading an existing system, or simply trying to understand the machine on your desk — the knowledge in this guide is your foundation. Technology evolves, but the core principles remain the same. Keep learning, keep building.
